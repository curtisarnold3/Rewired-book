# Chapter 17: Day 22 and Beyond

> **Companion app:** Work this protocol step by step at [startrewired.com](https://www.startrewired.com).

Congratulations. The installation is complete.

You’ve done 21 days of intensive behavioral reprogramming. You’ve proven to your nervous system that you can sustain this behavior. You’ve created new neural pathways and begun weakening the old ones.

But here’s what most people don’t understand: you’re only one-third of the way there.

Remember Philippa Lally’s research? Behaviors become truly automatic—requiring no willpower, no decision, no effort—after an average of 66 days. You’ve completed 21. You have roughly 45 more days before the new pattern becomes your default equilibrium.

This is the danger zone. This is where most people fail.

During the 21-day sprint, the intensity was its own protection. Every day was marked. Every behavior was tracked. The protocol demanded attention.

Now the protocol relaxes. And relaxation is where old patterns reassert themselves.

## The Maintenance Rules: Days 22-66

The maintenance phase has different rules than the installation phase.

**Rule 1: Reduce Intensity, Maintain Consistency.** During installation, you needed maximum intensity to create new neural pathways. During maintenance, you need consistent repetition to strengthen them. You no longer need to track every single behavior obsessively. But you DO need to maintain the core behaviors daily without exception. Miss one day, and the old pathways start reasserting themselves.

**Rule 2: Shift from FEAR to FATE.** During installation, you used the FEAR formula: Focus, Emotion, Agitation, Repetition. The agitation was necessary to break old patterns. During maintenance, shift emphasis to FATE: Focus, Authority, Tribe, Emotion. The patterns are installed—now you need social reinforcement to lock them in.

Specifically: Tribe becomes critical—surround yourself with people who expect the new you. Authority needs ongoing input—keep consuming content from people who embody your target identity. Focus remains constant—keep your arrival conditions visible. Emotion should shift from negative to positive—less harsh self-criticism, more pride in who you’re becoming.

**Rule 3: Weekly Check-Ins Replace Daily Tracking.** During installation: daily tracking, daily review, daily accountability. During maintenance: weekly review is sufficient. Every Sunday, ask yourself: Did I maintain the core behaviors this week? What almost derailed me? What do I need to adjust for next week?

## The Relapse Protocol

You will slip. Everyone does. The question is whether a slip becomes a slide.

**If you miss one day:** Return to the behavior immediately the next day. No self-punishment, no drama. One day doesn’t undo 21+ days of installation.

**If you miss two consecutive days:** This is a warning sign. Activate agitation again—something has gone wrong with your environment or emotional state. Do a mini-review: What changed? What triggered the slip?

**If you miss three or more consecutive days:** Your old patterns are reasserting themselves. Return to Week 1 of the installation protocol. Yes, really. The neural pathways need to be rebuilt.

This sounds harsh, but it’s based on the neuroscience: consistency matters more than intensity during the maintenance phase. Three missed days isn’t a minor slip—it’s evidence that the installation didn’t fully take.

## The 66-Day Graduation

Around Day 66, something shifts. The behavior that required intention during installation, and consistency during maintenance, starts feeling automatic.

You’ll notice it in small ways: You do the behavior without having to decide to do it. Skipping the behavior feels wrong, like forgetting to brush your teeth. The behavior is now your default, not your exception.

When you hit this point, the installation is truly complete. The new pattern is now part of who you are.

But stay humble. Old patterns never fully disappear—they just get weaker. Extreme stress, life disruptions, or environmental changes can reactivate them even years later. The maintenance mindset is forever: notice when you’re slipping, and catch it early.

## The Consumption Trap

Let me be direct about something: reading this book could actually make things worse.

Here's how. You now have sophisticated knowledge about why change is hard. You understand the neuroscience. You can explain the FATE model and the FEAR formula. You know about decision matrices and stress testing and identity lock-in.

This knowledge feels like progress. It produces the sensation of forward movement. You can discuss these concepts intelligently. You can explain to others why their change attempts fail.

But knowledge without execution is worse than ignorance.

The person who doesn't know why change is hard might stumble into effective approaches accidentally. The person who knows exactly why change is hard---but doesn't execute---uses that knowledge to justify continued inaction. They become sophisticated about their own stagnation.

"I can't change right now because I haven't done the proper threat modeling yet." "I need to optimize my environment before I start." "I should wait until I can do the full 21 days without interruption."

This is the consumption trap. You feel like you're making progress because you're acquiring knowledge. But knowledge acquisition is often a sophisticated form of procrastination---activity that feels productive without producing any actual change.

The only way out of the consumption trap is execution. Not more reading.

Not more preparation. Not more optimization. Execution.

## The Implementation Intention

Research by psychologist Peter Gollwitzer found that people who form "implementation intentions"---specific plans for when, where, and how they'll execute a behavior---are significantly more likely to follow through than people who simply intend to change.

The difference isn't motivation. It's specificity.

"I'm going to start the protocol" is a goal.

"I'm going to start the protocol on Monday at 6 AM by completing the Day 1 awareness audit, carrying my notebook with me all day, and recording at least 15 automatic behaviors before I go to sleep" is an implementation intention.

The goal leaves room for negotiation. The implementation intention closes negotiation before it starts.

So here's what I want you to do---not in general, but specifically:

## Your Immediate Next Actions

Complete the four pre-protocol exercises. Today. Not tomorrow. Not next week. Today.

1\. Define your arrival conditions with precision. What exactly does success look like? Not vague aspirations---specific, measurable states. "I weigh 175 pounds and can see muscle definition in my arms." "My savings account shows \\25,000." "I complete my priority task before checking email every workday." Write them down. Your

Reticular Activating System needs specific targets to filter for---give it something concrete.

2\. **Build Future A and Future B until they're vivid enough to feel.**

Future A: you in two years having executed successfully. Where are you? What does your body feel like? Who's around you? What are people saying? Future B: you in two years having continued your current patterns. Same questions, but this time make it uncomfortable. Make staying the same feel unsafe. Your amygdala responds to vivid threat 10x stronger than vague reward---use that asymmetry. Create both futures in sensory detail. Use photos, write detailed descriptions, create vision boards if that helps. The goal is emotional charge, not intellectual understanding.

3\. Write your identity document. Who is the person who would naturally execute this protocol? What do they believe about themselves, their time, their priorities? What are their default behaviors---the things they do without thinking? What standards do they never negotiate---the lines they never cross? Write it in present tense as if it's already true. This document becomes your daily anchor. You'll read it every morning during the protocol, reinforcing the neural pathways of who you're becoming.

4\. Audit your environment. What triggers pull you toward old behaviors? Digital: which apps, websites, notifications, accounts?

Physical: which locations, objects, routines? Social: which people, conversations, expectations? For each trigger, plan a specific intervention: delete, block, remove, rearrange, or disrupt.

Remember: context beats discipline. If you're relying on willpower to overcome environmental triggers, you've already lost. Change the environment so the right choices are easy.

Then pick your start date. Within the next seven days. Not "sometime soon." A specific date. Put it on your calendar with a reminder. Tell someone who will ask you about it---accountability makes the commitment real. Make it concrete enough that your brain treats it as an actual appointment rather than a vague intention.

Then execute. Day 1 through Day 21, without exception. Follow the protocol even when you don't feel like it. Especially when you don't feel like it. The protocol is designed to be uncomfortable because discomfort is the mechanism of change. If it felt easy, nothing would be happening.

## The Compound Effect of Starting

There's a reason I'm pushing you to start today, not tomorrow.

Every day you delay, the probability of starting decreases. Not because your circumstances change, but because your brain interprets delay as evidence that this isn't important enough to act on immediately.

You're training your brain right now. If you close this book and delay, you're training it to delay. If you close this book and execute the first pre-protocol exercise within the hour, you're training it to execute.

The first action is the hardest because it has no momentum behind it.

But it's also the most important because it creates the momentum that makes subsequent actions easier.

This is the compound effect of starting: the decision to act now makes future action more likely. The decision to delay makes future delay more likely. You're setting a trajectory, not just making a single choice.

So don't optimize for perfect conditions. Optimize for beginning. The protocol will refine itself as you execute. The conditions will never be perfect. The resistance will always have reasons to wait.

Start anyway.
