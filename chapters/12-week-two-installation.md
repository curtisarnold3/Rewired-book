# Chapter 12: Week Two: Installation

> **Companion app:** Work this protocol step by step at [startrewired.com](https://www.startrewired.com).

## Week Two: Installation

Days 8-14 Week Two is where most people fail.

And you won't.

The fundamental shift that happens this week:

**You stop using emotion as your decision-making authority.**

This doesn't mean ignoring emotions. It means demoting them. Emotion becomes data---information about your state. But data doesn't make decisions. Criteria make decisions.

This is what separates people who execute from people who merely have good intentions. The executor doesn't ask "what do I feel like doing?" They ask "what moves me toward my arrival conditions?"

Week One broke the automatic patterns. Week Two installs the new operating system.

## The Problem With "How Do You Feel?"

Our culture has made feelings the ultimate authority. "Follow your heart." "Trust your gut." "Do what feels right." This is terrible advice for behavior change.

Here's why: your feelings evolved to keep you safe and comfortable in an ancestral environment. They're optimized for short-term survival, not long-term flourishing. When your feelings say "don't do that hard thing," they're not wrong---they're just answering a different question than the one you're asking.

Your feelings are asking: "Is this comfortable? Is this safe? Does this require effort?" You need to be asking: "Does this move me toward what I actually want?" These questions often have opposite answers. The workout feels bad. The early wake-up feels bad. The difficult conversation feels bad. But they all move you forward.

Week Two installs a new decision-making architecture where criteria---not feelings---determine action.

## Day 8: The Criteria Shift

Today's practice:

For every decision today---and I mean every decision---ask yourself this question before acting:

"Does this improve my position or just satisfy an impulse?" Write down your answer before you act. Then decide based on the answer, not on how you feel.

Examples:

Should I check email right now? Does this improve my position or satisfy an impulse?

Should I have this snack? Does this improve my position or satisfy an impulse?

Should I engage with this conversation/debate? Does this improve my position or satisfy an impulse?

Should I keep working or take a break? Does this improve my position or satisfy an impulse?

The difficulty: Your brain will try to make impulses sound like strategy. "I need this break" when you're avoiding something hard. "This is important to stay informed" when you're just craving stimulation. The question cuts through the rationalization.

Track how many times you caught yourself about to act on impulse and redirected based on criteria instead.

## The Rationalization Machine

Your brain is remarkably good at making impulses sound reasonable.

Neuroscience research shows that decisions often happen in the emotional brain first, and the rational brain constructs justifications afterward.

You don't actually reason your way to checking social media---you feel the urge, act on it, and then your prefrontal cortex invents a reason ("I should see if that important message came through").

This is called post-hoc rationalization, and we all do it constantly.

The question "does this improve my position or satisfy an impulse?" disrupts this process by forcing the rational evaluation before the action rather than after.

When you actually pause and ask the question honestly, most rationalizations collapse. You know the difference between strategic action and impulse satisfaction. You just haven't been requiring yourself to check.

## Day 9: The Decision Matrix

Internal debate is wasted energy. Today we start eliminating it.

Think about how much time you spend arguing with yourself. Should I or shouldn't I? Maybe I should, but what if... This internal negotiation burns enormous mental energy and produces nothing.

\*\*Today we build a decision matrix---pre-decided responses for recurring situations.\*\* Step 1: List every recurring decision that currently requires you to think in the moment. Examples: When to check email. Whether to respond to non-urgent messages immediately. Whether to accept meeting invitations or social requests. What to do when you feel the urge to procrastinate. How to respond when someone tries to pull you into drama.

Step 2: For each situation, decide NOW what your response will be. Not "it depends"---an actual rule. Write it in if-then format:

IF someone asks me for something non-urgent, THEN I respond within 24 hours, not immediately.

IF I feel tired and want to quit early, THEN I complete my minimum output first.

IF I catch myself procrastinating, THEN I work for 10 minutes before deciding whether to continue.

*Step 3:* Write these rules down physically. Not just in your head.

This is implementation intentions in action---the research-backed technique of pre-deciding responses to specific situations. Studies show it dramatically increases follow-through because you're not relying on in-the-moment willpower (Gollwitzer 1999).

The principle: You're removing improvisation from areas where improvisation doesn't add value. Most recurring decisions don't need fresh thinking---they need consistent execution.

## Why Pre-Deciding Works

There's a phenomenon called decision fatigue. Every decision you make depletes a limited cognitive resource. By the end of a day filled with choices---what to eat, what to wear, how to respond to emails, whether to take that meeting---your decision-making quality degrades significantly.

This is why judges grant parole at much higher rates in the morning than in the afternoon. It's why you're more likely to eat junk food at night.

Your decision-making capacity is exhausted.

The decision matrix is a hack around this limitation. By pre-deciding your responses to recurring situations, you remove those decisions from the daily drain. You're not deciding whether to check email now---you decided that last week. You just execute the rule.

This preserves your limited decision-making capacity for situations that actually require fresh thinking.

## Day 10: Matrix Testing

Rules are easy when things are calm. Today we test them.

By now you have your decision matrix---pre-decided responses for recurring situations. But having rules isn't the same as following them when you don't want to.

\*\*Today's practice: Follow your decision matrix regardless of how you feel.\*\* This means:

When your scheduled email check time arrives but you're in the middle of something good---stop and check anyway. The rule is the rule.

When someone tries to pull you into drama but you're actually curious or irritated and want to engage---don't. The rule is the rule.

When you're tired and your minimum output isn't done---complete it anyway. The rule is the rule.

Track your compliance. How many times did your matrix apply today? How many times did you follow it versus override it?

The key insight: Feelings will tell you that "this time is different." This time there's a good reason to make an exception. This time the rule doesn't apply.

*It does apply. This time is not different.*

The moment you start making exceptions, you've reopened negotiation.

You're back to deciding everything in the moment based on emotion. The matrix becomes useless.

## The Identity Scaffold

Here's what's really happening when you follow your decision matrix even when you don't want to:

Your brain will violate logic before it violates identity. Right now, your identity is in flux---you're installing a new one. The decision matrix is the scaffolding that holds the new identity in place while it sets.

Every time you follow the rule despite not feeling like it, you're casting a vote for the new identity. Every time you make an exception, you're casting a vote for the old one. The identity that gets the most votes wins.

This is why compliance matters more than perfection. You don't need to feel like the person who follows these rules. You just need to act like that person, repeatedly, until acting becomes being.

## Day 11: External Tracking

How you feel is not how you're doing. Today we install real measurement.

Most people measure success by mood. Felt good today? Must be doing well. Felt bad? Something's wrong.

**This is backwards. Mood is noise. Behavior is signal.**

Today's assignment: Set up external tracking for the metrics that actually matter.

Create three measurement categories:

1\. Protocol Compliance: Did you execute your decision matrix? (Binary: yes/no for each rule.) 2. Output Completion: Did you produce what you said you'd produce today? (Binary: yes/no.) 3. Pause Success Rate: How many automatic impulses did you successfully pause before acting? (Count.)

Set up a simple tracking system. Paper works. A spreadsheet works. An app works. The format matters less than consistency.

Today's rule: At the end of today, fill in your tracking metrics. Then ask yourself: "According to my behavior, how did I do?" This answer might differ from how you feel. That's the point.

The principle: Success is doing what you said you'd do. Period. You felt terrible but executed perfectly? That's a successful day. You felt great but didn't follow your protocols? That's a failed day.

Disconnect feeling from assessment.

## The Hawthorne Effect

This is the Hawthorne effect again, from Chapter 8---people change their behavior the moment they know it's being measured. That's exactly the lever you're pulling with external tracking.

External tracking harnesses this effect deliberately. When you know you'll record your compliance at the end of the day, you're more likely to comply. The measurement itself changes the behavior.

This is why "I'll just try to do better" doesn't work but specific tracking does. Vague intentions produce vague results. Measured intentions produce measured results.

## The Scoreboard Principle

There's a related concept from sports psychology: teams that keep score play differently than teams that don't.

When you're just "playing around," effort is optional. When there's a scoreboard, every point matters. The game becomes real.

Your tracking system is your scoreboard. It transforms vague intentions into a game with actual stakes. Did you win today or not? The numbers tell you.

Some people resist this. They say it feels too rigid, too harsh, too unforgiving. They want to "give themselves grace" and "not be so hard on themselves." The problem: self-compassion without accountability is just permission to fail. The scoreboard isn't harsh---it's honest. It tells you what actually happened, not what you wish had happened or what you intended to happen.

You can handle the truth. You need the truth. The truth is how you improve.

## Day 12: Eliminating "Just This Once"

"Just this once" is the enemy. Today we kill it.

Here's how most people sabotage themselves: They're executing perfectly.

Following their protocols. Making progress. And then they think, "Just this once, I'll make an exception. Just this once won't matter." It matters.

"Just this once" reopens negotiation. It teaches your brain that rules are negotiable. And once rules are negotiable, you're back to debating every decision in the moment. Back to relying on willpower. Back to where you started.

Today's commitment: No exceptions to your protocols. None.

When you feel the urge to make an exception---and you will---notice it, label it ("there's the just this once pattern"), and execute the rule anyway.

Your internal script: "The rule exists to protect consistency.

Consistency is more important than optimization right now. I follow the rule." Track your "just this once" moments. How many times did your brain try to negotiate an exception today? How many times did you refuse?

The insight: Every time you refuse to negotiate, you're strengthening the installation. Every exception weakens it. The math is simple.

## The Compound Effect of Exceptions

Here's why single exceptions matter more than they seem:

Research on habit formation shows that consistency beats intensity.

Missing one day doesn't reset your progress significantly---but missing one day makes missing the second day much more likely. And missing two days makes the third almost inevitable.

The first exception isn't just one exception. It's permission for all future exceptions. It's a precedent that your negotiating brain will cite forever: "Remember that time we made an exception and it was fine?

Let's do that again." The "no exceptions" rule isn't about being rigid or perfectionistic.

It's about understanding how your brain uses precedent. The cleanest way to maintain the pattern is to never break it.

## Day 13: Closing Debate Loops

Your mind wants to keep arguing. Time to stop listening.

Even with a decision matrix, even with external tracking, you'll notice your brain still wants to debate. It generates options, counterarguments, "what ifs." It feels like being thoughtful.

*It's usually resistance.*

Today's practice: The moment a decision is made, the debate ends.

When you feel internal argument starting after a decision is made:

1\. Notice it ("there's the debate loop") 2. Say internally: "Decision is final. Execute." 3. Take action immediately No more processing. No more considering. The decision happened. Now there's only execution.

Why this matters: Internal debate is exhausting. It burns cognitive resources without producing results. And it's usually your brain trying to avoid discomfort by pretending to be careful.

Speed matters more than perfection here. A good decision executed immediately beats a perfect decision that gets debated into paralysis.

Track today: How many times did you catch yourself in post-decision debate? How many times did you successfully close the loop and execute?

## The Zeigarnik Effect

In the 1920s, psychologist Bluma Zeigarnik noticed that waiters could remember complex orders while they were being filled, but forgot them immediately after serving. She hypothesized that uncompleted tasks create a kind of mental tension that keeps them active in memory.

This became known as the Zeigarnik Effect: unfinished tasks occupy mental bandwidth until they're completed.

Internal debate is an unfinished task. When you keep a decision "open" for reconsideration, it continues consuming cognitive resources. Your brain keeps returning to it, generating new angles, rehearsing new arguments.

Closing the debate loop---declaring the decision final and moving to execution---releases that bandwidth. The mental tension resolves. You free up resources for actual work instead of endless rumination.

Remember: neurons that fire together wire together, but only with emotion. Every time you close the debate loop and execute anyway, you're wiring in the new pattern. You're telling your mammalian brain: we don't debate here. We decide and we move.

## Day 14: Week Two Integration

Week Two complete. Let's see where you are.

You've spent seven days installing criteria-based decision-making. Using emotion as data instead of authority. Pre-deciding responses. Tracking externally. Refusing exceptions. Closing debate loops.

This is the machinery of control. It's not glamorous. It's not inspiring. It's functional.

**Assessment questions (write your answers):**

1\. What's your average protocol compliance rate over the past week?

2\. What decision matrix rules are easy to follow? Which ones generate resistance?

3\. How has your internal debate changed? More quiet? Same noise?

4\. What "just this once" patterns keep trying to emerge?

5\. What's different about how you make decisions now versus two weeks ago?

Adjustment time: Look at your decision matrix. Any rules that don't make sense anymore? Any situations that keep arising that need a pre-decided response? Revise the matrix based on what you've learned.

## What You've Actually Built

Let me be explicit about the infrastructure you've installed this week.

You've created a decision-making system that doesn't require motivation.

The matrix executes regardless of how you feel. The tracking runs regardless of your mood. The "no exceptions" rule operates regardless of your energy level.

This is the point. Motivation is unreliable. It comes and goes based on sleep, stress, blood sugar, weather, and a hundred other variables you don't control. Any system that depends on motivation will fail when motivation fails.

The system you've built doesn't depend on motivation. It depends on compliance with rules you set when you were thinking clearly. Future-you follows the rules that past-you created. That's it.

This might feel mechanical. It might feel like you're just going through the motions. That's fine. Going through the motions is how motions become automatic. The feeling of authenticity comes later---after the behavior is installed.

Prepare for Week Three: Here's what's coming---the new patterns get tested under deliberate stress. Not waiting for stress to find you.

Creating it intentionally.

Why? Because it doesn't matter if your protocols hold when things are calm. What matters is whether they hold when you're tired, pressured, and everything is going wrong.

If the patterns survive extreme stress, they'll survive normal stress.

That's the principle.

Rest tonight. You've built something. Week Three proves whether it's real.
